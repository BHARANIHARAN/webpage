SolarWinds Cyberattack (2020)

Overview: One of the most significant cyberattacks in history, where hackers, believed to be state-sponsored, compromised the Orion software by SolarWinds. The attackers inserted malicious code into a routine software update, affecting thousands of organizations, including U.S. government agencies and major corporations. Impact: The attack allowed hackers to spy on internal systems for months without detection, leading to a major cybersecurity review in the U.S. government and widespread industry ramifications.

Microsoft Exchange Server Hack (2021)

Overview: Hackers exploited vulnerabilities in Microsoft Exchange Server, which affected over 250,000 organizations globally. The attackers, believed to be linked to China, gained access to emails and other sensitive data. Impact: The breach had far-reaching consequences, with many businesses forced to patch their systems to avoid further exploitation. The U.S. and U.K. governments issued joint advisories to mitigate the attack.

Viruses

Trojan Horses

Overview: Trojan horses disguise themselves as legitimate software or files to trick users into downloading them. Once activated, they can steal data, delete files, or provide remote access to attackers.

Examples: Emotet: Initially a banking trojan, it evolved into a major delivery mechanism for other forms of malware, including ransomware. Zeus: A trojan that primarily targets banking information, often used in large-scale fraud schemes. Dangers: Data theft, financial fraud, system compromise, and acting as a gateway for other malicious software like ransomware.

Rootkits

Overview: Rootkits are tools used to gain unauthorized access to a computer and maintain privileged access over time. They can be difficult to detect because they are designed to hide their presence.

Examples: Stuxnet: A highly sophisticated rootkit that targeted industrial systems, specifically the Iranian nuclear program, causing physical damage to nuclear centrifuges. Dangers: Undetectable access to systems, remote control by hackers, data exfiltration, and manipulation of system processes without detection.

Phishing and Social Engineering

Overview: Phishing involves tricking individuals into providing sensitive information like login credentials or financial data through fraudulent emails or websites. Social engineering involves manipulating individuals into divulging confidential information.

Examples: Business Email Compromise (BEC): A form of phishing where attackers impersonate a company executive to trick employees into wiring funds or revealing sensitive information. Spear Phishing: A targeted form of phishing where attackers customize emails to a specific individual or organization. Dangers: Identity theft, financial loss, unauthorized access to sensitive systems, and data breaches.

Cryptojacking

Overview: Cryptojacking involves hackers using someone else's computing resources to mine cryptocurrencies. This can happen when users unknowingly run mining scripts embedded in websites or software.

Examples: Coinhive: A mining script used by cybercriminals to hijack computing power and mine Monero cryptocurrency. Dangers: Reduced system performance, high energy consumption, and damage to hardware due to prolonged mining activity.
